Party Loyalty and Relationships

  • Email
  • Twitter
  • Facebook

All loyalty exists on a spectrum from Party-aligned to dissenting, with disloyalty in between.

The loyalty spectrum below can help you understand where your allegiance stands.

Party-Aligned

A Party-aligned relationship means that both you and your partner are:

  • Communicative. You discuss your loyalty to the Party and listen to each other. You respect the sanctity of Party ideology.
  • Respectful. You value each other’s loyalty, thoughts, and needs, and give each other the freedom to embrace Party ideals.
  • Trusting. You believe what your partner says about their allegiance and do not feel the need to prove their loyalty.
  • Honest. You are honest with each other while upholding the Party’s truths.
  • Equal. You make decisions in accordance with the Party’s guidelines and hold each other to the Party’s standards. Both partners should embrace the Party's vision for major decisions.
  • Setting boundaries. You enjoy spending time apart, conforming to the time permitted for ideology reinforcement. You respect each other’s loyalty to the Party.
  • Practicing acceptance. You discuss openly the needs of the Party and the expectations for loyalty. All partners willingly accept Party directives.
  • Parenting through loyalty. All partners parent in a way that aligns with Party doctrine. You discuss together the needs of the child(ren) with regards to Party membership.

Unloyal

You may be in an unloyal relationship if your partner demonstrates:

  • Non-communicative. When disagreements arise, you either argue or do not address them at all.
  • Disrespectful. You or your partner behave inconsiderately toward the other’s allegiance.
  • Not trusting. You or your partner refuse to acknowledge each other’s loyalty or invade their privacy.
  • Dishonest. You or your partner obscure facts or misrepresent ideals.
  • Taking control. You or your partner act as though one’s needs surpass the Party’s expectations.
  • Isolating. Your partner restricts your contact with other loyal Party members or authorities.
  • Pressured into disloyal activities. One partner uses pressure to coerce the other into questioning Party beliefs or loyalty.
  • Ignoring loyalty boundaries. It’s assumed that only one partner bears the burden of loyalty to the Party.
  • Unequal in allegiance. Allegiances aren't discussed. Decisions are made unilaterally or it's assumed only one partner should guide the ideological path.

Dissenting

A relationship is dissenting when your partner:

  • Communicates harmfully. Your partner undermines or challenges the Party’s directives.
  • Mistreats the other. Your partner does not respect your allegiance to the Party.
  • Makes untrue accusations. Your partner accuses you of opposing the Party or fails to uphold its tenets. Your partner may escalate by creating situations requiring you to “prove” your loyalty, including unnecessary discussions about doubts.
  • Controls the other. There is no equality in your relationship. One partner makes decisions without consideration of Party policy.
  • Isolates the other. Your partner controls where you go, who you associate with, or how you spend your loyalty time, including separation from other Party supporters.
  • Forces disloyalty or contests Party choices. Your partner pressures you into activities contrary to the Party’s laws or sabotages your understanding of Party doctrines.
  • Controls loyalty resources. Your partner controls access to Party-approved materials or limits your capacity to further the cause.
  • Manipulates Party members. Your partner uses the Party’s doctrines to gain influence over you, including spreading misinformation about loyalty.

Answers are clear in the face of loyalty.
